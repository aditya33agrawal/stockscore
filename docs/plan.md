# Blog Plan — "The Mean Reversion of Everything"

## Working title options

Pick one (or remix):

1. **The Mean Reversion of Everything** — borrows a trader's term, broadens it to a life-philosophy. Clean, memorable, sounds like a book.
2. **The Profit-Booking Principle** — keeps the stock-market origin in the title; intriguing because most readers won't expect it to apply to life.
3. **What Goes Up: A Theory of Everyday Gravity** — softer, more poetic, hints at the gravity tie-in.
4. **The Old Things Stay** — leans into the carpentry/forging insight; quiet and confident.
5. **Spike and Return** — short, rhythmic, almost like a law of physics.

**Recommendation:** *The Mean Reversion of Everything*, with a subtitle like *"What a stock chart taught me about life, careers, and why carpenters will outlast software engineers."* The title does the hooking, the subtitle does the promising.

---

## Author voice & framing

- First-person, conversational, but with the weight of someone who has *noticed* something. Not academic.
- Acknowledge the theory is informal — that's part of the charm. Don't oversell it as proven; sell it as a *lens*.
- Anchor every abstract claim in a concrete picture (a chart, an analogy, a story). Readers should be able to *see* the curve in their head by the end of each section.

---

## Structure (sections of the blog)

### 1. Hook — Open with the chart
Start with a vivid image: a stock that spikes 40% in a week, and then, inevitably, gives most of it back. Ask the reader: *why does this happen, every single time?* Tease that the answer isn't really about stocks at all.

### 2. The mechanism — Why spikes come back
Explain profit-booking in plain English: when a crisis hits, people don't sell what's losing — they sell what's *winning*, because that's where the gains are locked in. The biggest winners get cut down first. Meanwhile the boring stocks stay boring, untouched, and quietly outlast the storm.

This is the seed of the theory. Name it here — call it **the Profit-Booking Principle** or **Spike-and-Return**.

### 3. The leap — This isn't about stocks
Pivot: this same pattern shows up everywhere. Whenever something rises faster than it "should," forces accumulate to pull it back.

### 4. Examples (the core of the post — make these visceral)

Suggested examples to expand the user's original list:

- **Gravity** — what goes up comes down. Trite, but worth keeping as the warm-up.
- **A human life** — peak strength, looks, energy in your 30s–40s; before and after, a gentler curve. Almost a perfect normal distribution.
- **Empires** — Rome, the British Empire, the Mongols. The faster they rose, the harder they fell. The Holy Roman Empire took its time and lasted a thousand years.
- **Viral fame** — internet celebrities who blow up in a week are forgotten in a month. Slow-built reputations (Morgan Freeman, Warren Buffett) compound for decades.
- **Trends and fashion** — bell-bottoms, fidget spinners, NFTs. Things that "everyone" suddenly does, almost no one will do in five years. Meanwhile jeans (invented 1873) just keep going.
- **Companies** — meteoric IPOs (WeWork, Pets.com) collapse; boring 100-year-old businesses (Coca-Cola, Nestlé, Tata) compound quietly forever.
- **Diets and fitness fads** — keto, paleo, juice cleanses. Each peaks and recedes. "Eat less, move more" has been right for a thousand years and will be right for another thousand.
- **Cities and neighborhoods** — boomtowns (Detroit, gold-rush towns) crash hardest. Cities that grew slowly over centuries (Kyoto, Varanasi, Istanbul) just keep existing.
- **Relationships** — the most intense, fast-burning romances often don't last. The slow ones that started as friendship tend to.
- **Songs and movies** — #1 hits forgotten in a year; a Beatles song from 1965 still plays in every café.
- **Scientific hype cycles** — cold fusion, blockchain-everything, "AI winter" cycles. The Gartner Hype Curve is literally this theory drawn out.
- **Personal income/career growth** — a sudden promotion or windfall is often followed by stagnation or regression to baseline.

The user should pick 4–6 of these for the actual post — too many examples dilutes the punch. Mix scales: one cosmic (gravity), one personal (a life), one historical (empires), one cultural (trends), one professional (the career one — which is the punchline).

### 5. The deeper claim — Age as a predictor of longevity
This is the most original part of the theory and deserves its own section.

> The longer something has already existed, the longer it will likely continue to exist.

This is the **Lindy Effect** — a real, named idea (Mandelbrot, popularized by Nassim Taleb). The blog should *mention this by name* — it gives the theory intellectual backing without diluting originality, because the user's framing (profit-booking as the mechanism) is genuinely a fresh angle on it.

Then make the user's career argument:

- **Carpentry, forging, farming, mining, masonry, cooking, healing** — millennia old. Will still be here in 2525.
- **Finance / advisory** — 5,000 years old (Mesopotamian temple loans). Probably here for another 5,000.
- **Software engineering** — 70 years old. Currently spiking. By the logic of the theory: hasn't peaked yet, but the peak is coming, and after that, a long descent toward "just another trade."
- **Influencer / content creator** — 15 years old. Spike phase. The curve says: enjoy it while it lasts.

Frame it carefully — not as doom for software engineers, but as a recognition that *no spike is permanent*, and that the oldest professions are oldest precisely because they solve problems humans will always have: shelter, food, tools, money, healing.

### 6. The caveat — Where the theory doesn't (cleanly) apply
A good theory states its own limits. Mention:

- Some things genuinely don't revert — extinction, knowledge, certain technologies (we're not going back to candles). The theory is about *attention, valuation, and popularity*, not raw capability.
- Survivorship bias works in reverse here: we remember the old things *because* they survived. The carpenters who didn't last left no signal.

Including this section makes the whole piece feel more honest and earns the reader's trust.

### 7. Close — What do you do with this?
End with the *use* of the theory:

- Be suspicious of anything rising too fast — including your own success.
- Be patient with anything growing slowly — including yourself.
- When picking a career, a craft, a habit, a partner — *ask how long this thing has already existed*. Bet on the old.
- The world rewards spikes, but it remembers slopes.

Land on a single resonant line. Something like:

> *Everything that rises must converge. The question is only whether you bought in at the spike, or you were the one quietly compounding the whole time.*

## Interactive graph ideas (for the webpage)

These should reinforce the theory visually. Suggested in priority order:

1. **The signature chart** — a stylized stock-style line that spikes and reverts. Let the reader drag a "shock event" slider; the spike pulls back faster the higher it went. This is the *visual thesis* of the post and should sit at the top, under the title.

2. **The career-longevity timeline** — a horizontal timeline of professions (carpentry, finance, medicine, engineering, software, content creation) showing how long each has existed. Hover any of them to see a projected forward curve (per Lindy). Sits in section 5.

3. **The bell curve of a life** — a clean normal distribution with milestones placed on it (peak strength ~30, peak earnings ~50, etc.). Sits in section 4.

4. **Spike-vs-slope comparison** — two lines on the same axes: one steep spike that crashes, one gentle slope that quietly passes the spike's final value after enough time. The visual punchline of the whole post.

5. *(Optional, advanced)* — let the reader type in a thing ("TikTok", "Roman Empire", "blue jeans") and the page plots a stylized Lindy curve for it based on a hardcoded "age" lookup. Gimmicky, but very shareable.

Use the same chart library already in the project (Recharts, based on `components/FinancialCharts.tsx`) so it fits visually.

## Tone and length

- **Length:** 1,500–2,200 words. Long enough to feel substantial, short enough to finish in one sitting.
- **Tone:** quietly confident. Don't hedge every sentence, but don't oversell either. Let the examples do the persuading.
- **Avoid:** academic jargon, excessive citations, "as we shall see," "in conclusion." This is a personal essay, not a paper.

---

## Suggested writing order

1. Draft the examples section first — it's the most fun and unlocks the voice.
2. Then the careers section — it's the most original and deserves the most care.
3. Then the open and close — these matter most for whether people share it.
4. The mechanism and caveat sections last — they're scaffolding.

## Open questions for the author

- Do you want to name the theory after yourself (e.g. "Aditya's Law")? Bold but memorable. Or keep it descriptive?
- Should the post end with a call-to-action (subscribe, comment, share your own example) or just land the final line and stop? The latter is more elegant.
- Are you comfortable invoking the Lindy Effect by name, or do you want the post to feel fully self-originated? Recommendation: name it — credibility goes up, originality of your *mechanism* (profit-booking) is undiminished.
